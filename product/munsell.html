Munsell's Color theory

Albert Munsell’s color theory originated from classifying soil types for the USDA, and is optimized for human perception. It’s similar to the HSV (hue, saturation, value) model of color theory present in many softwares.

Most people know enough about color theory to understand the color wheel. But if we expand the color wheel to include all dark and light colors (value), and a range from bright to dull (saturation, or chroma), we can theorize all human-perceptible color as a 3D color space. There are theories of color spheres, color cylinders, color cubes, or in the case of Albert Munsell, an irregular 3D geometry he called a color tree.

You can imagine the trunk of the tree having a black base and a white top. Radiating from this trunk are the hues of the color wheel: red, orange, yellow, yellow-green, green, and so on. The brightest (most saturated) leaves of each hue branch is on the outside of the tree; each step inward, the hue gets grayer.

Some branches are long, and some branches are short. The reason they’re irregular is that Munsell’s optimized his color theory for human perception. Imperceptible steps necessary to make a perfect cylindrical tree were left out. The yellow branches are much longer at the top of the tree, where it’s lighter; the blue branches are much longer at the base of the trunk, where it’s darker.

This theory of color is similar to HSV, or Hue Saturation Value models of color used in many softwares. Munsell, however, used the term “chroma” and “chromaticity” instead of “saturation.”
